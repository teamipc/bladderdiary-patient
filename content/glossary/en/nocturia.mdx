**Nocturia** is the medical term for waking from sleep with the need to urinate, and then actually urinating. Once a night is normal at most adult ages, and almost universal by age 70. The threshold that turns waking up to pee into a clinical concern is **two or more times most nights**.

Nocturia is a symptom, not a disease. It splits cleanly into two paths with completely different causes:

- A **bladder problem**: the bladder asks to be emptied at smaller volumes than it should ([overactive bladder](/learn/glossary/overactive-bladder), [BPH](/learn/bph), [pelvic floor](/learn/glossary/pelvic-floor) dysfunction, irritation).
- A **kidney problem**: the kidneys make too much urine while you sleep, called [nocturnal polyuria](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria). Common causes include heart failure, sleep apnea, late-day diuretics, and uncontrolled diabetes.

A 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) answers a yes-or-no question that tells you which path is yours: is the urine you make from bedtime to first morning void more than a third of your daily total? If yes, the kidney path. If no, the bladder path.

For the full breakdown of causes, treatment, and what the diary shows, see [why you wake up to pee at night](/learn/nocturia).
