The **nocturnal polyuria index (NPi)** is the share of your 24-hour urine output that is produced overnight, between bedtime and your [first morning void](/learn/glossary/first-morning-void). It's the single most useful number on a [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) for deciding whether [nocturia](/learn/nocturia) is from the bladder or from the kidneys.

The math: divide the urine you produce from bedtime to first morning void (FMV included) by your total 24-hour output. Multiply by 100 for a percent.

The thresholds, set by the International Continence Society in 2018:

- **NPi over 33%** in adults over 65: [nocturnal polyuria](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria).
- **NPi over 20%** in younger adults: nocturnal polyuria.

If your NPi is above the threshold, the kidneys are the lead actor. The bladder is just delivering the message. Treatments aim upstream: treat sleep apnea, optimize heart-failure medications, move diuretic timing, use compression stockings during the day, or in selected cases use desmopressin under clinical supervision.

If your NPi is below the threshold but you still wake two or more times a night, the bladder is the lead actor. The treatments are different: [bladder training](/learn/bladder-training) drills, [BPH](/learn/bph) treatment in men, pelvic-floor physical therapy, sometimes bladder-targeted medication.

A note on NPi calculation:

- The FMV is included in the numerator, not the daytime total. This is because the FMV represents urine produced during sleep that just happened to come out after waking.
- A bedtime void (just before sleep onset) is counted as the last daytime void, not part of the night.
- Day 1 of a 3-day diary is excluded from NPi calculations in the IPC framework, because the first day often doesn't reflect a baseline pattern.

Without an accurate NPi, nocturia treatment ends up aimed at the wrong organ. With it, the right path is usually obvious within a single clinic visit.
