The **average voided volume (AVV)** is the typical volume of urine per bathroom trip. It's computed from a 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) by dividing total urine output by the number of voids in the period.

Typical ranges:

- **Healthy adult**: about 240 to 350 mL on most trips. Roughly the size of a coffee mug.
- **Small AVV**: under 200 mL on most voids. Points to a storage problem or high sensitivity.
- **Large AVV**: over 500 mL on most voids. Points to delayed sensation or habitual over-holding.

A note on AVV calculation in the IPC (Integrated Pelvic Care) framework: a double void (two trips close together that empty as one) counts as **two voids**, not one, in the denominator. AVV is meant to reflect what a typical trip looks like. If you went twice in 5 minutes and combined the volume, the math should still see two trips.

What AVV tells you:

- **Small AVV with normal [maximum voided volume](/learn/glossary/maximum-voided-volume)** means the bladder can hold a normal amount when you give it time, but on most trips it's asking to be emptied early. Classic [overactive bladder](/learn/glossary/overactive-bladder) pattern.
- **Small AVV with small MVV** means the [functional capacity](/learn/glossary/functional-bladder-capacity) itself is reduced. The training looks different.
- **Normal AVV with high [frequency](/learn/glossary/urinary-frequency)** points away from bladder problems and toward high fluid intake or polyuria.
- **Large AVV with low frequency** is normal in some adults, but worth checking against [post-void residual](/learn/glossary/post-void-residual) to make sure the bladder isn't quietly retaining urine.

Day 1 of a 3-day diary is excluded from AVV calculations in the IPC framework, because people often shift behavior on the first day of tracking. AVV is computed across days 2 and 3.
