**LUTS** stands for **lower urinary tract symptoms**: an umbrella term for any symptom involving the bladder, urethra, and surrounding structures. It is what clinicians use to talk about urinary complaints before any diagnosis is settled.

LUTS splits into three buckets:

- **Storage symptoms** (the bladder is asking too often): [frequency](/learn/glossary/urinary-frequency), [urgency](/learn/glossary/urinary-urgency), [nocturia](/learn/glossary/nocturia), urge-related leaks.
- **Voiding symptoms** (something is in the way of getting urine out): weak stream, hesitancy, intermittency, straining, terminal dribble.
- **Post-void symptoms** (something is wrong at the end): sense of incomplete emptying, post-void dribble.

Knowing the mix matters more than the label. Storage-dominant LUTS points to bladder, pelvic-floor, or irritation drivers. Voiding-dominant LUTS in men over 50 most often points to [BPH](/learn/bph). Both together point to long-standing obstruction with a secondary bladder problem.

LUTS is not specific to one diagnosis. The same symptom set can come from BPH, [overactive bladder](/learn/glossary/overactive-bladder), pelvic-floor dysfunction, [nocturnal polyuria](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria), recurrent urinary tract infections, or chronic cystitis, among others. A 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary), a urinalysis, and (in men) a basic prostate exam are usually the first three steps. The diary is the cheapest and the most informative.

You will see "LUTS" on a clinic note or referral letter often. It does not mean a clinician hasn't figured out what's going on; it means they are describing the symptoms before naming the diagnosis. The diagnosis comes from the diary, the exam, and a few targeted tests.
