**Nocturnal polyuria** is overproduction of urine at night by the kidneys. Normally the kidneys produce less, more concentrated urine while you sleep, because the antidiuretic hormone (ADH) rises overnight. In nocturnal polyuria, that overnight ADH peak is blunted or overwhelmed, and the kidneys keep making daytime-volume urine through the night.

The clinical definition uses the [nocturnal polyuria index](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria-index) (NPi), which is the share of your 24-hour urine output produced from bedtime through your [first morning void](/learn/glossary/first-morning-void):

- **NPi over 33%** in adults over 65, or
- **NPi over 20%** in younger adults

Common causes:

- **Age-related decline in ADH** — the most common driver in older adults.
- **Heart failure or chronic venous insufficiency** — fluid pooled in the legs during the day returns to circulation when you lie down.
- **Obstructive sleep apnea** — apnea events trigger a hormonal signal that tells the kidneys to dump salt and water.
- **Late-day diuretics** — moving the dose to morning often resolves the problem.
- **Uncontrolled diabetes** — high blood sugar pulls water into the urine.
- **Heavy daytime water intake** that overwhelms the night-time ADH signal.

Nocturnal polyuria is the kidney path of [nocturia](/learn/nocturia). Bladder treatments do not fix it. The right moves are upstream: treat the sleep apnea, optimize heart-failure medications, move diuretic timing, use compression stockings during the day.
