**Urge incontinence** is leaking urine immediately after a sudden, hard-to-defer urge to urinate. The leak happens because the bladder muscle contracts strongly before you can reach a toilet. It's a feature of [overactive bladder (OAB)](/learn/glossary/overactive-bladder), which often produces urgency without leaking, but can progress to leaking when the urge gets ahead of the trip.

This is different from **stress incontinence**, where the leak happens with a cough, sneeze, lift, or laugh. The mechanisms are different and the treatments are different:

- **Urge incontinence** is bladder-led. The bladder is the problem. Behavioral training and medications that calm the bladder muscle are first-line.
- **[Stress incontinence](/learn/glossary/stress-incontinence)** is support-led. The urethral sphincter and pelvic-floor muscles do not hold up under pressure. Pelvic-floor physical therapy is first-line.

People often have **mixed incontinence**, where both patterns are present. A 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) and an honest pad-test help separate the two.

What helps with urge incontinence:

- The [urge-suppression drill](/learn/bladder-training/urge-suppression-techniques): freeze, contract pelvic floor, slow breathe, walk calmly. Reframes the urge as a wave, not a cliff.
- A 14-day cut of caffeine, alcohol, and other [bladder irritants](/learn/bladder-irritants/foods-that-irritate-the-bladder). Often a meaningful piece of the picture.
- Pelvic-floor physical therapy. The floor is part of urge control too, not just stress incontinence.
- Bladder-targeted medication (anticholinergics, beta-3 agonists) when behavioral measures plateau.

If you are leaking, the diary is the single most useful thing to bring to a first clinical visit. It turns "I leak sometimes" into a specific pattern a clinician can match to a treatment.
