**Stress incontinence** is leaking urine when something raises pressure inside the abdomen: a cough, sneeze, lift, laugh, jump, or running step. There is usually no urge before the leak. The mechanism is a [pelvic floor](/learn/glossary/pelvic-floor) and urethral sphincter that cannot rise to the moment of pressure.

It's the most common type of incontinence in younger and middle-aged women, often after pregnancy, vaginal delivery, or pelvic surgery. In men, stress incontinence is most often a complication of [prostatectomy](/learn/post-prostatectomy/pee-a-lot-after-surgery) or other pelvic surgery.

This is different from **[urge incontinence](/learn/glossary/urge-incontinence)**, where leaking follows a sudden urge. The two are sometimes mixed, but the treatments are different.

What helps with stress incontinence:

- **Pelvic-floor physical therapy** is first-line. Trained PFPTs can confirm whether the floor is under-recruited (Kegel-style strengthening helps) or over-tight (relaxation work is what helps). Doing the wrong direction of exercise can stall progress for months.
- **Lifestyle modifications.** Weight management, treating chronic cough, and avoiding constipation all reduce intra-abdominal pressure and help.
- **Pessary devices** for women, or external compression devices, can support the urethra during high-pressure activities.
- **Surgical procedures** (sling, urethral bulking, artificial urinary sphincter) are options when conservative measures plateau and quality of life is meaningfully affected.

Pure stress incontinence does not respond to bladder-targeted medications: those treat the wrong organ. A 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) plus a frank conversation about which activities trigger leaks gives a clinician what they need to direct the workup.
