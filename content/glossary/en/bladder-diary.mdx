A **bladder diary** (also called a voiding diary or frequency-volume chart) is a structured record of your bladder activity over a fixed period, usually three days. For each void, you record the time and the volume in milliliters or fluid ounces. You also record drinks, leaks, and any urgency notes.

Three days is the standard because it's long enough to capture your real pattern and short enough that you actually finish it. A single day is too noisy. A week is more than most people complete honestly.

What you need:

- A way to measure volume. A clear plastic cup with marked ounces or milliliters works. A measuring cup from the kitchen works. The exact value matters less than measuring consistently.
- A way to record. A printable [PDF](/learn/bladder-diary/bladder-diary-pdf) is the standard. An [app](/learn/bladder-diary/bladder-diary-app) like My Flow Check makes the math automatic.
- Three consecutive days where you can be home for most of the day. Not three perfect days. Real ones.

What you get out of three days:

- **[24-hour voided volume](/learn/glossary/24-hour-voided-volume) (24HV)**: total daily urine output. Healthy adults sit around 1.5 to 2 liters.
- **[Average voided volume](/learn/glossary/average-voided-volume) (AVV)**: typical volume per trip. Healthy adults sit around 240 to 350 mL.
- **[Maximum voided volume](/learn/glossary/maximum-voided-volume) (MVV)**: largest single void, a proxy for [functional bladder capacity](/learn/glossary/functional-bladder-capacity). Healthy adults sit around 400 to 500 mL.
- **[Nocturnal polyuria index](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria-index) (NPi)**: share of urine output produced overnight. Above 33% in adults over 65 is [nocturnal polyuria](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria).

Those four numbers tell a clinician what kind of bladder problem (or kidney problem) is in front of them within minutes. Without them, the first visit is mostly guessing. With them, the right path is usually obvious.
