**Urinary frequency** is going to the bathroom more often than typical. Most adults urinate 6 to 8 times in a 24-hour period. Above that, on most days, is what clinicians call frequency.

Frequency is one of the most common bladder symptoms. It's not a diagnosis on its own, it's a signal that something is driving the increase. The seven most common drivers:

- **Fluid intake.** Drinking 3 liters or more in a day can shift voiding upward in a perfectly healthy bladder. The diary catches this immediately.
- **Caffeine and bladder irritants.** Coffee, tea, soda, alcohol, and acidic juices irritate the bladder lining and act as mild diuretics. A 14-day [elimination test](/learn/bladder-irritants/foods-that-irritate-the-bladder) tells you whether this is your driver.
- **[Overactive bladder (OAB)](/learn/glossary/overactive-bladder).** The bladder asks to be emptied at lower volumes than it should.
- **[BPH (enlarged prostate)](/learn/bph).** In men over 50, the prostate narrows the urethra and the bladder works harder, eventually getting twitchy.
- **Pelvic-floor dysfunction.** A floor that's too tight, too weak, or poorly coordinated produces frequency.
- **[Polyuria](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria) (high total urine output).** Diabetes, water-loading, or kidney issues can produce a lot of urine, which leads to a lot of trips.
- **Bladder lining irritation.** Chronic cystitis, interstitial cystitis, or after-effects of pelvic radiation.

A 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) sorts out which driver is yours within minutes of looking at the chart. Total volume, [average voided volume](/learn/glossary/average-voided-volume), and timing patterns each point to a different cause and a different fix.
