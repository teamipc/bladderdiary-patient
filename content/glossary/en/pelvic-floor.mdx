The **pelvic floor** is the muscular layer that forms the base of the pelvis. It runs from the pubic bone in front to the tailbone in back and from one sit-bone to the other, like a hammock. It supports the bladder, the bowel, and (in women) the uterus and vagina, and (in men) wraps around the urethra and rectum.

The pelvic floor has three jobs:

1. **Support** the pelvic organs against gravity and intra-abdominal pressure.
2. **Control continence** by maintaining baseline tone in the urethral and anal sphincters.
3. **Coordinate timing** with the bladder and bowel, relaxing when you want to urinate or have a bowel movement, contracting when you want to hold.

When the pelvic floor doesn't do those three jobs well, you get pelvic-floor dysfunction, which shows up in many forms:

- **Underactive pelvic floor.** Floor is too weak. Produces [stress incontinence](/learn/glossary/stress-incontinence), pelvic organ prolapse in women, post-prostatectomy leaks in men. Strengthening (Kegel-style) is the right direction.
- **Overactive pelvic floor.** Floor is too tight, often without you realizing it. Produces [urgency](/learn/glossary/urinary-urgency), [frequency](/learn/glossary/urinary-frequency), pelvic pain, painful intercourse, hesitancy, and incomplete emptying. Strengthening makes it worse. Relaxation work is the right direction.
- **Poorly coordinated pelvic floor.** Floor and detrusor don't time together properly. Produces hesitancy, intermittent stream, and incomplete emptying.

A trained pelvic-floor physical therapist can tell which pattern you have on a focused exam. Doing the wrong direction of exercise can stall progress for months. This is why many of the [bladder training](/learn/bladder-training) drills include pelvic-floor work, and why a referral to a pelvic-floor PT is often the most useful single referral for chronic bladder symptoms.

For women and men both, the pelvic floor is part of the system the bladder lives inside. The diary tells you about the bladder; the floor exam tells you about the rest.
