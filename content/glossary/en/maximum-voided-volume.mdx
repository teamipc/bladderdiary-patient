The **maximum voided volume (MVV)** is the largest single void you record across a 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary). It's a clean, real-life proxy for [functional bladder capacity](/learn/glossary/functional-bladder-capacity), the volume your bladder routinely holds before it asks to be emptied.

Typical ranges:

- **Healthy adult**: 400 to 500 mL on the largest void. Often this is the [first morning void](/learn/glossary/first-morning-void).
- **Reduced capacity**: under 300 mL. Indicates a real storage problem, not just sensitivity.
- **Very large**: over 600 mL consistently. Can suggest delayed sensation or chronic overstretching.

A note on MVV calculation in the IPC (Integrated Pelvic Care) framework: do **not** combine the volumes of a double void (two trips close together that empty as one) when computing MVV. Use each individual recorded volume. Combining double voids inflates the MVV and hides a small-capacity pattern. (For 24-hour total volume, double voids are combined; for MVV, they are not. The two metrics answer different questions.)

Why MVV matters:

- MVV separates [overactive bladder](/learn/glossary/overactive-bladder) (small [average voided volume](/learn/glossary/average-voided-volume), normal MVV) from a structurally small bladder (small AVV AND small MVV).
- A normal MVV with low AVV means the capacity is there and the problem is sensation calibration. Sensation training drills work well.
- A small MVV across the diary is the signal for a defunctionalized or genuinely small bladder. The training program looks different.
- MVV before and after a [BPH](/learn/bph) treatment course tracks how much bladder function recovers as obstruction is relieved.

A clinician reads MVV and AVV together. AVV tells them what your bladder is doing on most trips. MVV tells them what it is capable of when given time to fill.
