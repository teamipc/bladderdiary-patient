The **24-hour voided volume (24HV)** is the total urine you produce in a 24-hour period. It's one of the four numbers a 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) computes, and it's the first number a clinician looks at.

Typical ranges:

- **Healthy adult**: about 1.5 to 2 liters per day. Driven by intake, age, kidney function, and ambient temperature.
- **Polyuria** (high output): consistently over 2.5 to 3 liters per day. Common drivers: high water intake, uncontrolled diabetes, certain medications, kidney concentrating problems.
- **Low output**: consistently under 1 liter per day. Usually points to dehydration, but can also reflect kidney issues.

24HV in the IPC (Integrated Pelvic Care) framework is computed bedtime to bedtime, with day 1 of a 3-day diary excluded as an adaptation period. The reason: people often change their behavior on the first day of tracking. Days 2 and 3 represent baseline.

Why 24HV matters:

- High 24HV with [nocturnal polyuria](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria) suggests global polyuria, not just a nighttime problem. Diabetes and water-loading are the common drivers.
- Normal 24HV with high nighttime fraction is classic age-related nocturnal polyuria.
- Low 24HV with [frequency](/learn/glossary/urinary-frequency) points to a small bladder making many small voids, not a kidney problem.
- 24HV calibrates fluid-intake recommendations: a person making 1 liter a day is dehydrated; one making 3 liters a day can probably cut intake.

The 24HV is the simplest diary number to interpret and the most informative single value at the first clinic visit. Together with [average voided volume](/learn/glossary/average-voided-volume), [maximum voided volume](/learn/glossary/maximum-voided-volume), and the [nocturnal polyuria index](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria-index), it tells the clinician what kind of bladder problem (or kidney problem) you're dealing with.
