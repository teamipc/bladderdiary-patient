**Post-void residual (PVR)** is the volume of urine left in the bladder immediately after you finish urinating. A normal PVR is small: under 50 mL is generally considered normal, 50 to 200 mL is a gray zone, and over 200 mL is meaningful retention worth investigation.

PVR is measured two ways:

- **Bladder ultrasound (preferred).** A handheld scanner over the lower abdomen reads the residual volume in seconds. Painless, no infection risk.
- **Catheter (older method).** A thin catheter drains the residual urine. Used when ultrasound isn't available or the volume needs to be exact.

A high PVR points to one of two patterns:

- **Outlet obstruction.** Something downstream of the bladder is blocking flow: most often [BPH](/learn/bph) in men, urethral stricture, severe pelvic organ prolapse in women, or post-surgical scarring.
- **Bladder under-activity.** The bladder muscle ([detrusor](/learn/glossary/detrusor)) doesn't contract strongly enough to fully empty. Causes include diabetes-related nerve damage, certain medications (anticholinergics, opioids), spinal-cord conditions, and long-standing untreated obstruction that has worn the muscle out.

Why PVR matters:

- **Recurrent UTI risk** rises with retained urine.
- **Upper-tract pressure** can build over time when the bladder routinely fails to empty, eventually affecting kidney function.
- **Treatment choice** changes. A man with BPH and a low PVR can usually start with medications. A man with BPH and a PVR over 300 mL is closer to a procedural decision.

PVR is not part of a [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary), but is often the first imaging-based test ordered alongside the diary, especially for men over 50 with voiding-dominant symptoms.
