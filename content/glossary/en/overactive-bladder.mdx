**Overactive bladder (OAB)** is a clinical syndrome defined by sudden, hard-to-defer [urgency](/learn/glossary/urinary-urgency), usually with [frequency](/learn/glossary/urinary-frequency) and often with [nocturia](/learn/glossary/nocturia). Some people also have urge-related leaking, which is called urge incontinence.

The underlying mechanism is a [detrusor](/learn/glossary/detrusor) (bladder muscle) that contracts at lower volumes than it should. Many things cause that twitchiness: nerve signaling problems, post-surgical changes, irritation from caffeine or medications, [BPH](/learn/bph) in men, and pelvic-floor dysfunction. OAB can happen at any age, though it gets more common after 50 in both men and women.

What helps:

- **Behavioral training first.** The 2023 Cochrane review of bladder training found clear, durable improvement comparable to first-line medications, with far fewer side effects. The four [bladder training](/learn/bladder-training) drills cover the main patterns.
- **Caffeine and bladder irritants.** A 14-day [elimination test](/learn/bladder-irritants/foods-that-irritate-the-bladder) tells you whether dietary triggers are part of the picture.
- **Medications.** Anticholinergics (oxybutynin, solifenacin) and beta-3 agonists (mirabegron, vibegron) target the bladder muscle. These are typically layered on after behavioral training, not before.
- **Pelvic-floor physical therapy** when the floor is the contributing system.

A 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) shows whether your symptoms fit OAB or something else. The diary numbers, especially [average voided volume](/learn/glossary/average-voided-volume) and [maximum voided volume](/learn/glossary/maximum-voided-volume), are what a clinician uses to confirm the diagnosis and pick the right next step.
