**Urinary urgency** is a sudden, hard-to-defer feeling that you need to urinate right now. It's different from a normal "I should go soon" cue: urgency is sharp, often startling, and hard to walk through.

Urgency is the lead symptom of [overactive bladder (OAB)](/learn/glossary/overactive-bladder), but not all urgency is OAB. Other drivers:

- **Bladder irritation** from caffeine, alcohol, citrus, carbonated drinks, or specific medications. Often improves within 2 weeks of an [elimination test](/learn/bladder-irritants/foods-that-irritate-the-bladder).
- **Urinary tract infection.** Sudden new urgency with burning or fever is a UTI workup.
- **[BPH](/learn/bph) in men over 50.** An obstructed bladder eventually becomes a twitchy bladder.
- **Pelvic-floor dysfunction.** A tight or poorly coordinated [pelvic floor](/learn/glossary/pelvic-floor) generates urgency-like signals.
- **Nerve signaling problems** after pelvic surgery, spinal injury, or with neurological conditions.

Urgency is what makes a fast trip to the bathroom turn into [urge incontinence](/learn/glossary/urge-incontinence) when the urge gets ahead of the trip.

What helps:

- The [urge-suppression drill](/learn/bladder-training/urge-suppression-techniques) reframes the urge as a wave that crests and falls. Freeze, contract pelvic floor, slow breathe, walk calmly. The wave passes.
- [Bladder training](/learn/bladder-training) extends the interval between voids gradually, retraining the urge signal over weeks.
- Cutting [bladder irritants](/learn/bladder-irritants/foods-that-irritate-the-bladder) for 14 days, then reintroducing one at a time.
- Pelvic-floor physical therapy when the floor is part of the picture.

A 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) plus an urgency rating per void (mild, moderate, severe) gives a clinician a real picture of how often urgency hits and at what bladder volumes. That pattern points to the right next step.
