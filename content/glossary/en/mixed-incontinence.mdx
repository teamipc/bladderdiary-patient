**Mixed incontinence** is when both [urge incontinence](/learn/glossary/urge-incontinence) and [stress incontinence](/learn/glossary/stress-incontinence) are present at the same time. People leak from sudden urges they couldn't outrun, AND from coughs, sneezes, lifts, or laughs. This is common in women over 50 and in men after pelvic surgery.

The two components have different causes and different treatments:

- **Urge component**: a [detrusor](/learn/glossary/detrusor) that contracts at low volumes. Behavioral training and bladder-targeted medications help.
- **Stress component**: a [pelvic floor](/learn/glossary/pelvic-floor) and urethral sphincter that don't hold up under pressure. Pelvic-floor physical therapy and (for severe cases) surgical procedures help.

The clinical move in mixed incontinence is to figure out which component is dominant, then go after that one first. A 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) plus an honest pad-test usually answers the question:

- More leaks tied to urgency (the bathroom door felt too far away)? Urge-dominant. Start with [urge-suppression](/learn/bladder-training/urge-suppression-techniques) and a bladder-targeted approach.
- More leaks tied to coughs, sneezes, jumps, or laughs? Stress-dominant. Start with pelvic-floor PT.
- Roughly even? Often the urge component improves more rapidly with training, so it's often treated first, with a stress-focused track running in parallel.

Mixed incontinence sometimes makes people give up because nothing seems to fully fix it. The reality is most people get meaningful improvement on both components when each is treated for what it is. Treating both as if they were one problem (a common mistake) leaves both partially treated.

If you leak, the diary is the cheapest, fastest way to clarify which component is doing what. It also gives you something concrete to bring to your first clinical visit.
