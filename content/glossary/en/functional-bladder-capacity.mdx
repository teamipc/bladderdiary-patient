**Functional bladder capacity** is the largest volume your bladder routinely holds before you feel the need to empty it. It's a real-life number, not a maximum-stretch number from a urodynamic test. The cleanest way to estimate it is to look at the **[maximum voided volume](/learn/glossary/maximum-voided-volume) (MVV)** across a 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary): the largest single void in three days, which is a good proxy for what your bladder can comfortably hold.

Typical ranges:

- **Healthy adult**: around 400 to 500 mL on the largest void of the day, often the [first morning void](/learn/glossary/first-morning-void).
- **Reduced capacity**: under 300 mL on the largest void of the day. Indicates a real storage problem.
- **Very large capacity**: over 600 mL consistently. Can suggest delayed sensation or chronic overstretching, sometimes from years of habitually holding it.

Why functional capacity matters:

- It separates [overactive bladder](/learn/glossary/overactive-bladder) (small average voids, but capacity is fine) from a structurally small bladder (small average voids AND small maximum).
- In men with [BPH](/learn/bph), reduced functional capacity often improves once obstruction is treated, because the bladder regains normal storage behavior.
- It tells a [bladder training](/learn/bladder-training) program what's actually trainable: a normal MVV with low [average voided volume](/learn/glossary/average-voided-volume) means the capacity is there and the problem is sensation calibration, not structure.

A bladder that habitually voids small (because of fear of leaks, or from years of an obstructed flow) can lose functional capacity over time. Clinicians sometimes call this a defunctionalized bladder. The good news: it's often reversible with patient training, gradually extending the interval between voids. The diary is the input that makes this trainable.
