The **detrusor** is the smooth-muscle layer of the bladder wall. It's the muscle that contracts to push urine out when you urinate. The rest of the time, it relaxes and stretches as the bladder fills, which is what gives the bladder its storage function.

Detrusor behavior shows up under three labels in clinic:

- **Normally functioning.** Stays relaxed during filling, contracts smoothly when you choose to void.
- **Overactive.** Contracts at lower volumes than it should, producing the [urgency](/learn/glossary/urinary-urgency) and [frequency](/learn/glossary/urinary-frequency) of [overactive bladder](/learn/glossary/overactive-bladder). On a urodynamic test, this looks like spontaneous contractions during the filling phase. Common causes: nerve signaling problems, irritation, long-standing [BPH](/learn/bph) obstruction, post-surgical changes.
- **Underactive.** Doesn't generate enough force to fully empty the bladder. Produces a high [post-void residual](/learn/glossary/post-void-residual), weak stream, and a sense of incomplete emptying. Causes: diabetes-related nerve damage, certain medications, spinal-cord conditions, or a detrusor that has been worn out by years of pushing against an obstruction.

Most bladder-targeted medications act on the detrusor. Anticholinergics (oxybutynin, solifenacin) and beta-3 agonists (mirabegron, vibegron) calm an overactive detrusor. There is no good medication that strengthens an underactive one, which is why bladder under-activity is harder to treat than overactivity.

The detrusor's behavior cannot be measured directly with a [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary), but it can be **inferred**. Small [average voided volume](/learn/glossary/average-voided-volume) with normal [maximum voided volume](/learn/glossary/maximum-voided-volume) suggests an overactive detrusor with intact capacity. Small voids across the board suggest reduced [functional capacity](/learn/glossary/functional-bladder-capacity), often from long-standing detrusor changes. The diary plus a 30-second post-void ultrasound covers most of what a clinician needs at the first visit.
