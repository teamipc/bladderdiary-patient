**BPH** stands for **benign prostatic hyperplasia**: non-cancerous enlargement of the prostate gland. The prostate sits below the bladder and surrounds the upper urethra. As it enlarges, it narrows the urethra, the bladder has to push harder, and over time the bladder muscle becomes thick and twitchy. BPH is one of the most common conditions in men over 50 and present in some form in most men over 70.

The symptoms split into two groups:

- **Storage symptoms**: [frequency](/learn/glossary/urinary-frequency), [urgency](/learn/glossary/urinary-urgency), [nocturia](/learn/glossary/nocturia), urge-related leaks.
- **Voiding symptoms**: weak stream, hesitancy, intermittency, straining, sense of incomplete emptying.

The mix tells you what's going on: storage-dominant symptoms point to a twitchy bladder driven by long-standing obstruction. Voiding-dominant symptoms point to current obstruction. Most men over 50 have a mix.

What helps:

- **Lifestyle and behavioral.** Spacing fluids, cutting evening fluids, [bladder training](/learn/bladder-training) drills, avoiding bladder irritants. Often enough for mild symptoms.
- **Medications.** Alpha-blockers (tamsulosin, alfuzosin) relax the prostate and bladder neck; results in days. 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride) shrink the prostate over months. Combination is common.
- **Procedures and surgery** when medications plateau and symptoms are meaningfully affecting life: minimally invasive options (Rezum, Urolift) and traditional surgery (TURP, simple prostatectomy).

BPH is not the same as prostate cancer. The two can coexist, which is why men with new urinary symptoms typically get a PSA test along with the symptom workup. A 3-day [bladder diary](/learn/bladder-diary) is the single most useful thing to bring to the first clinical visit. It tells the clinician whether your symptom mix points to storage, voiding, or both, and whether [nocturnal polyuria](/learn/glossary/nocturnal-polyuria) is also part of the picture.
